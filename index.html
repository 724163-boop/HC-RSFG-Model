Theory Overview

The Puzzle of Red Galaxies in the Early Universe

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has discovered a population of extremely red, compact objects in the early Universe (redshift z ≈ 5–7), known as Little Red Dots (LRDs). Their vivid red colours have sparked intense debate: are they powered by accreting supermassive black holes (AGN), heavily dust‑obscured star formation, or something else entirely?

Existing explanations struggle to account for several observed facts. The AGN hypothesis cannot easily explain X‑ray quiet LRDs or those showing only narrow emission lines. The dust‑obscured star‑formation scenario is challenged by reports of dust‑free LRDs at z > 7. These discrepancies suggest a third possibility: the stellar population itself may be intrinsically red.

The HC‑RSFG Solution: Born Red

The Hierarchical Core Red Star‑Forming Galaxy (HC‑RSFG) theory proposes that the extreme redness of LRDs—and of many other galaxies across cosmic time—is a natural consequence of an extremely bottom‑heavy Initial Mass Function (IMF) operating in the dense cores of early galaxies.

The cooling timescale is approximately 30,000 times shorter than the free‑fall timescale. The Jeans mass is maintained below 1.5 M☉ — the RSFG mode is physically permitted by fundamental atomic physics.

A Unified but Flexible Evolutionary Picture

The HC‑RSFG mechanism can act in different environments and epochs, naturally producing a variety of observed galaxy types. One possible evolutionary sequence is:

  • Dark‑RSFG (z ≈ 7–10): extremely faint, currently undetected.
  • Little Red Dots (z ≈ 5–7): the bright, compact phase observed by JWST.
  • Red Misfits (z ≈ 0.5): galaxies that are still forming stars yet appear red.
  • Relic Galaxies (z ≈ 0): massive ellipticals such as NGC 1277 that preserve a bottom‑heavy IMF in their cores.

Testable Predictions & Observational Support

  • LRD‑like colours (F200W − F444W > 1.0 mag) are achieved without any dust.
  • Emission lines are extremely weak (EW < 1 Å) because massive stars are absent.
  • The central mass‑to‑light ratio of relic galaxies is elevated, consistent with a bottom‑heavy IMF.
